# Frequency Versus Volume: The hypertrophy debate where the evidence has moved under our feet

I had a 47-year-old recreational bodybuilder on a classic four-day upper/lower split. He'd plateaued on hypertrophy for 12 weeks despite pushing volume into the 20+ set per muscle group per week range. Here's what we changed.

For the better part of a decade, the working coach's operating procedure for hypertrophy was straightforward: get your weekly volume in, and how you distribute it matters less than the total. The landmark meta-analysis by Schoenfeld, Ogborn, and Krieger (2016) seemed to settle it — higher weekly volumes produced greater hypertrophy, with a dose-response relationship that kept climbing past 10 sets per muscle group per week. The practical takeaway most coaches absorbed was simple: more volume equals more growth, so prioritize total weekly volume and let frequency fall where it may.

But the evidence base has shifted substantially since 2016, and if you're still programming volume-first without considering frequency distribution, you may be leaving adaptations on the table — or worse, burying clients in junk volume they can't recover from.

## Where the literature stood

Schoenfeld et al. (2016) was a foundational meta-analysis, but it had important limitations coaches should keep in mind. The analysis included studies with a wide range of training statuses, age groups, and intervention lengths. Many of the included studies lasted only 6–12 weeks — a timeframe where dose-response relationships may look linear but flatten considerably over longer training periods.

The population matters here. If you're coaching untrained or early-intermediate clients, the volume-hypertrophy relationship is more forgiving. Novel stimuli drive growth almost regardless of how you organize the work. But for trained lifters — the population most coaches are actually working with once the easy gains are gone — the picture is more complicated.

## The plot twist: frequency as a volume moderator

A subsequent meta-analysis by Schoenfeld, Grgic, and Krieger (2019) specifically examined training frequency's effect on hypertrophy. The finding that made headlines was that training a muscle group twice per week produced superior hypertrophy to once per week. But the nuance buried in the data was more interesting: when weekly volume was equated, frequency effects diminished substantially — but didn't disappear entirely.

This suggested frequency wasn't just a delivery mechanism for volume. It appeared to be an independent, if modest, driver of hypertrophy. The mechanism is likely related to muscle protein synthesis (MPS) kinetics. Damas et al. (2016) demonstrated that MPS peaks approximately 24–48 hours post-training in trained individuals, returning to baseline within 48–72 hours. If you're hitting a muscle once every 5–7 days, you're spending significant time in an MPS-neutral state. Twice-weekly stimulation keeps the signaling window open more consistently.

For my 47-year-old client, this was the lever. We redistributed his 20 weekly sets per muscle group from two marathon sessions into four moderate sessions across a higher-frequency template. Same volume. Different distribution.

## What I see in practice

Within three weeks on the redistributed protocol, he reported improved training quality across the board — subjective, I know, but backed by measurable load increases in the 6–12 rep range that had been stagnant for months. By week eight, ultrasound measurements showed a 4.2mm average increase across four measured sites (chest, quads, lateral deltoid, upper arm), compared to essentially zero change in the prior 12-week block.

This is a single case. I'm not generalizing from it. But it aligns with what the frequency literature predicts and what I see repeatedly in masters lifters: when recovery capacity is the limiting factor — and it often is for athletes over 40 — spreading volume across more sessions allows for higher per-set quality and better cumulative stimulus.

## The contested ground: how much volume is enough?

This is where it gets genuinely messy, and where honest coaches need to hold multiple positions simultaneously.

Schoenfeld and Krieger (2019) updated the volume analysis and found that hypertrophy continued to increase up to 20+ sets per muscle group per week, but with markedly diminishing returns beyond 10–12 sets. The effect sizes were: 2–3 sets per week (ES = 0.41), 4–6 sets (ES = 0.55), 7–9 sets (ES = 0.66), and 10+ sets (ES = 0.80). These are not trivial jumps.

However — and this is critical — these effect sizes collapsed substantially when only trained individuals were analyzed. The dose-response curve flattened. A 2023 study by Androulakis-Korakakis et al., which specifically examined trained lifters doing 6 versus 10 versus 20 sets per muscle per week, found no significant hypertrophy differences between 10 and 20 sets over eight weeks.
